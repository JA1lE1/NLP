Depending on what aspect of topic modeling you are interested, I'd recommend a handful of papers.

Introductory paper
Probabilistic topic models by David Blei
A very good review paper with a very nice high-level explanation of topic models. It also provides a good sense of the present and future research directions.
http://www.cs.columbia.edu/~blei...

Seminal paper
Latent Dirichlet Allocation by Blei, Ng & Jordan
This is the paper that introduced Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) - one of the most widely adopted method to build topic models. It is nicely structured with the constant use of examples from text analysis and a progressive level of difficulty. I particularly like the chronological history in the introduction. It accounts very nicely for the research progress made over the past 30 years.
http://www.cs.columbia.edu/~blei...

Interpreting topic models
Reading Tea Leaves: How Humans Interpret Topics Models by Chang, Boyd-Graber, Gerrish, Wang & Blei
This papers sheds light on one of the biggest problems brought up by topic models: evaluation. While likelihood can be an easy way to compare the predictive power between different models, it is not suited to assess the semantic correctness of the resulting topics. This paper provides some interesting perspectives on these issues and offers a handful of quantitative methods to perform a semantic evaluation of topics.
http://papers.nips.cc/paper/3700...

If you're interested in LDAs in particular, I'd suggest starting off with the following

  • Latent Dirichlet Allocation DM Blei, AY Ng, MI Jordan - the Journal of machine Learning research, 2003 (http://machinelearning.wustl.edu...)
  • Daniel Ramage, David Hall, Ramesh Nallapati, and Christopher D. Manning. 2009. Labeled LDA: A supervised topic model for credit attribution in multi-labeled corpora. In Proceedings of the 2009 Conference on Empirical Methods in Natural Language Processing. (http://nlp.stanford.edu/pubs/lld...)
  • For a more general overview, you might want to read https://www.cs.princeton.edu/~bl...

If you want to do experiments with LDAs, i'd suggest using the Stanford's CoreNLP toolkit. (The Stanford NLP (Natural Language Processing) Group)

Hope this helps.
